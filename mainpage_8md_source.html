4 Introduction:
5 -------------
6 
7 The objective of this project is, given data from a single frame, to identify players in a basketball game, compute
8 their positions on the field and classify them into players belonging to two teams (TeamA and TeamB) and the Referees.
9 Data is provided in the form of 3D cloud points (X,Y,Z) associated to a color triplet (R,G,B). @ref Fig1 shows a 3D
10 view of the input cloud points.
11 
12 The problem is divided into steps as follows:
13 - Identifying the players on the field is achieved by applying a spacial clustering algorithm with some care taken
14 for noise suppression.
15 - Classify the players into the three classes by analysing the color of the pixels within each cluster.

29 Algorithms Description:
30 -----------------------
31 
32 ### Identifying players on the field:
33 
34 A spacial clustering in the horizontal (X,Z) plane is performed to identify the positions of every player on the field.
35 
36 The clustering algorithm consists of the following steps:
37 - @b pre-clustering: This step is intended to speed-up the following steps by first grouping together
38  data points that are very close to each other. The ouput of this step is the input for the actual
39  clustering algorithm.
40 - <b> Density calculation </b>: a measure of density is needed for the following steps. The high granularity of the data allows
41  to implement a density measure which does not require a kernel by simply counting the number of data points in the
42  vicinity of the point of interest.
43 - @b Seeding: seeds are defined as local maxima of density. In order to reduce noise, only seeds with densities above a
44  certain threshold are selected. A good choice of this threshold was found to be at half of the maximum density on the
45  entire field. @ref Fig2 shows the density map in the (X,Z) plane and the corresponding seeds positions.
46 - @b Clustering: Once the seeds are found, the clustering is performed by assigning each data point to the nearest seed.
47 - @b Cleaning: The final step consist of removing noise by stripping outlier data points. This is particularly important
48  when performing the surface color analysis to identify the teams to which each player belongs. This cleaning is done
49  by selecting data points within a certain number of standard deviations (in 2D) from the centroid of the cluster.
50  Thanks to the high granularity of the data, an aggressive selection with a threshold of 1 &sigma; can be used.
51  @ref Fig3 shows the result of the clustering as well as the effect of the noise removal.

70 ### Identifying the teams:
71 
72 Two approaches were implemented, one of which uses supervised while the other uses unsupervised learning.
73 Both approaches use the same set of discriminating features.
74 
75 
76 #### The Discriminating Features:
77 
78 After the players are identified on the field using spacial clustering, the color of their uniform can be used to classify
79 them into three categories: two teams (TeamA and TeamB) and the Referees. The RGB color components of pixels in each cluster
80 provide some discrimination (@ref Fig4 (a)), but it is not enough by itself at as the data presents a high spread and therefore
81 a large overlap between the three classes.
82 
83 Instead, each cluster is divided into 5 vertical layers, and the average color components for each layer are used.
84 The set of discriminating variables is therefore composed of 15 (3 RGB x 5 Layers) variables. This ensures taking into account
85 the correlation between the color information and the position within the cluster which can be seen in @ref Fig4 (b).

97 #### Training and evaluation data sets:
98 
99 The input data is randomly split into a training and an evaluation sample:
100 - <b> Evaluation data: </b> Consists of 20% of the input data. The high granularity of the input data allows
101  for such an assymmetric spllitting.
102 - <b> Training data: </b> Consists of the remaining 80% of the input data. After spacial clustering, a bootstrap
103  approach is used to generate a larger training sample. Each cluster is resampled with replacement into 300 clusters
104  each containing approximately an equivalent number of points as the corresponding evaluation clusters.
105  This is necessary to ensure the stability of the classification algorithm.
106 
107 
108 #### Supervised Approach:
109 
110 The supervised approach assumes that the user manually analyse few frames of data to provide a training samples with "truth"
111 information. The trained algorithm can then be applied to rest of the frames for tracking purposes.
112 
113 In this project, a multi class boosted decision tree (BDT) is implemented with three outputs (one output for each of "TeamA",
114 "TeamB" and "Referees" as shown in @ref Fig4). A Gradient boosting algorithm is used along with shrinkage and bagging for
115 regularization.
116 A reasonable set of default BDT parameters is chosen based on literature and prior experience. In fact, it is usually the case
117 that the best BDT performance is achieved for a large number of short trees (weak learners) with slow boosting (low shrinkage).
118 There were no optimization attempted specifically for this project as the performance of the default parameters were
119 satisfactory and more importantly, such optimiztion requires a larger training data set which is completely statistically
120 independent from the evaluation sample.
121 The main parameters include:
122 - @b NTrees = 200: Number of trees in the forest, chosen at the lowest value giving stable results to optimize for execution speed.
123 - @b nCuts = 20: Sampling of the input variables range while scanning for optimal cuts during individual trees training.
124 - @b MaxDepth = 2: Limits the depth of each tree in the forest.
125 - @b Shrinkage = 0.30: Shrinkage parameter controling the learning "spead" of the boosting procedure.
126 - @b BaggedSampleFraction = 0.50: Fraction of training data used in each step of the boosting procedure.

141 #### Unsupervised Approach:
142 
143 A fully unsupervised approach assumes no input from the user except the data itself. It should discover the
144 classification by analysing the data features. The drawback is that the algorithm is unable to label the categories
145 as "TeamA", "TeamB" and "Referees". Therefore, the algorithm needs to be assisted by forcing the number of categories
146 to 3 and labeling the category with the least elements as "Referees".
147 
148 In this project a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is implemented. The 15 inputs variables are transformed using PCA
149 and the leading 3 components are retained. The higher order components present little to no discriminating power as can be
150 seen in @ref Fig6.
151 In this reduced phase space, a k-means clustering algorithm is implemented to perform the actual classification (@ref Fig7).
152 The fact that k-means algorithm require the final number of clusters to be known in advance is often quoted as one of
153 the limitations of the algorithm. In this appllication, it is actually a desired feature.
154 
155 The parameters of the PCA transformation are computed using the bootstrapped training data. The evaluation data is
156 then added to the training data to perform the k-means clustering on both data sets simulatenously. This significantly
157 increase the stability of the clustering output.

192 Further Discussions:
193 --------------------
194 
195 Several aspects of this project can still be improved. In the following I briefly discuss some of these aspects.
196 
197 ### Data set for training:
198 
199 The data used for training in this project is highly corelated with the evaluation data and the BDT is overtrained.
200 In fact, if for example one player from TeamA is labeled as TeamB during training, it is highly probable that this
201 player would be labeled wrongly during the classification.
202 
203 This can be solved by training on data from several frames taken at separate times during a game.
204 With a larger sample of training data statistically independent from the evaluation data,
205 a thorough optimization of the MVA algorithms can be performed as well as testing other MVAs such as Recurrent
206 Neural Networks or even Deep Learning.
207 
208 ### Few assumtions:
209 
210 In this project, a few assumptions were made and taken for granted which if not valid, might break the whole
211 algorithm. In particular, the players are assumed to be standing up right and in contact with the floor and fully
212 inside the field of vision.
213 
214 A player sitting on the floor is likely to be treated as noise, while a player performing a jump will have the
215 color vs. layer data corrupted. Also, a player who is standing halfway inside and outside the field of vision
216 is higly likely to be treated as noise.
217 
218 All these aspects can be the subjects for further improvements.
219 
220 ### Speed considerations:
221 
222 Several measures were taken to improve the speed of execution. Most of these are merely good coding practices such as
223 passing arguments by reference, and caching results of expensive operations, etc... Others impose modifications to
224 the algorithms themselves, in particular:
225 - The pre-clustering step is completely unnecessary in terms of the output of the algorithm but it speeds up the
226 clustering step by a factor of 50 in this particular case.
227 - The speed of execution of the BDT increases significantly with the number and depth of the decision trees in the BDT.
228 These parameters were reduced to the minimum values that yield stable results. As an example, the execution time
229 was seed up by a factor 10 with no visible impact on performance by reducing the depth of the trees from 4 to 2 and
230 reducing the number of trees in the forest from 1000 to 200.
